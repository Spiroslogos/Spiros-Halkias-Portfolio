Microbial Fuel Cells // April 23rd, 2026

Background

The ethylene oxide production facility located at 8404 River Road, Geismar, Louisiana 70734 operates via an oxygen based direct oxidation process with a production capacity of 220,000 metric tonnes per year. In this process, ethylene and molecular oxygen react over a silver-based catalyst at 200-300 degrees Celsius and pressures between 1-3 MPa. The plant emits approximately 1 kg CO2 per ethylene oxide, yielding 220,000 metric tonnes of CO2 per year in total process emissions. Of these, roughly 95% are released as pure CO2 from the CO2 desorber unit. The remaining 5% is split between flares and process heaters.

Microbial Fuel Cells Reducing CO2 Footprint

The proposed Microbial Fuel Cell (MFC) system does not attempt to capture or convert the 220,000 MT of CO2 emissions from the ethylene oxide plant. Rather, the MFC is set to target the CO2 equivalent emissions attributable to the plant’s workforce through their daily activities. The MFC reduces the plant’s overall carbon footprint in three ways:

Energy displacement. The MFC generates electricity from the biological oxidation of biomass in worker wastewater, offsetting grid electricity consumption. Louisiana has an SRSO grid emission factor of 0.371 kg CO2/kWh, higher than the average regional grid in the U.S.

Wastewater treatment substitution. By replacing the energy intensive conventional treatment of sludge, the MFC avoids significant indirect CO2 emissions associated with the plant’s staff and infrastructure.

Clean water recovery. The MFC effluent achieves 80-95% chemical oxygen demand (COD) removal, producing water suitable for reuse in plant operations, thereby reducing demand for treated water from municipal supply. This means that the MFC consumes 80-95% of organic matter, and the remaining exits as effluent.

The MFC is appropriate for this plant because it is scalable, requires no external energy input, and poses no threat or risk. Furthermore, the plant’s nearly pure CO2 byproduct stream could serve as a cathode electron acceptor in a specific MFC configuration, converting CO2 directly into useful chemical feedstock. However, for simplicity purposes this paper only reviews energy displacement, wastewater treatment substitution, and clean water recovery.

Literature Review

MFC technology has been studied as a wastewater treatment and energy recovery method since the early 2000s. It has been demonstrated that a single chamber MFC can generate up to 26 mW/m^2 of power while removing 80% of COD from wastewater. A significant milestone was achieved in 2016 when a 200 liter modularized MFC system began operation at a municipal wastewater treatment plant for several months, reporting a normalized energy recovery (NER) of 0.36 kWh per kg of COD removed and confirming operation.

MFC performance across multiple configurations has been noted, showing that wastewater fed systems typically yield 40-54 W/m^3 volumetric power density, while glucose fed systems can reach up to 130-316 W/m^3. It has been reported that an MFCs energy recovery is 0.216-0.630 kWh/kg COD compared to the 0.60 kWh/kg COD consumed by conventional activated sludge. This represents a net energy advantage of over 1.2 kWh per kg of COD treated.

From a greenhouse gas perspective, an estimated CO2 reduction potential of 0.006-4.35 kg CO2 per cubic meter of wastewater treated is attainable by an MFC. The U.S. operational carbon footprint for centralized wastewater treatment has been quantified at 0.344-0.46 CO2/m^3, establishing a baseline for this study’s offset calculations.

Scope of the Project

This study is scoped as follows. Emissions are quantified across all daily activity categories. A design and mass flow balance of an on site MFC system that treats worker generated sanitary wastewater is included. Analysis of MFC performance, as well as a determination of a minimum workforce size for MFC viability. All emissions and factors are specified in Louisiana. Out of scope data includes, CO2 desorber streams, lifecycle for MFC hardware, and worker behavior changes.

Assumptions

All calculations rely on the following assumptions, which represent literature supported values.

Each worker is an average American adult commuting by private gasoline car.

The round trip commute is 25.9 miles per day, for an emission factor of 404 g CO2/mile.

The average American mixed diet has an emission factor of 5.5 kg CO2/person/day.

A single person household has an emission factor of 3.5 g CO2/person/day.

Office and on site energy including HVAC and lighting has an emission factor of 1.34 kg CO2/worker/day.

Per capita water use of 340 L/day, and wastewater generated at 180L/day, resulting in a water supply emission factor of 0.46 kg CO2/m^3.

Consumer goods and waste disposal has an emission factor of 1.5 CO2/person/day.

Digital and streaming has an emission factor of 0.10 kg CO2/person/day.

MFC substrate uses 180L/worker/day at COD = 300 mg/L.

Louisiana grid: 0.371 kg CO2/kWh.

MFC NER: 0.36 kWh/kg COD removed, Coulombic efficiency of 24.8%, COD removal of 80%.

No embodied carbon of MFC materials is included.

A single family household is assumed, as treating family households veers too far from the ethylene oxide plant for the scope of this project.
