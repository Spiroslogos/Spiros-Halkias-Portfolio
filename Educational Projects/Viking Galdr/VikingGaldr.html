Viking Galdr // December 5th, 2025

Introduction

Galdr was a form of magical chanting and singing prevalent during the Viking age (c. 800-1100 CE) that represented an intersection of sound and spiritual beliefs in pre-Christian Norse societies. Unfortunately written music from this era did not survive; our understanding of Galdr relies heavily on linguistic evidence, archeological findings, and descriptions within old Norse sagas and poetry. This practice was not just a musical performance but a ritual rooted in personal development and exerting influence over fate. What I look to answer is the following: How did Galdr function as a connection between sound and belief in these societies? I argue that Galdr does this by allowing practitioners to invoke spiritual energy and communicate with the divine, through the power of spoken word. Modern reconstructions of Galdr exist, such as those performed by Kjell Braaten, which allows contemporary listeners to experience the enduring bridge between the voice and the sacred (Braaten).

Broad Sociocultural Context

The broader religious/cultural context of Galdr is rooted in Norse paganism, a polytheistic faith spanning Scandinavia and Viking settlements from the Late Iron Age, through to the Middle Ages. During this time it was a common belief the cosmos was composed of Nine Worlds connected by the world tree Yggdrasil. These worlds included the realms of the gods, humans, giants, and the dead. The constant flow of energy and influence between these realms meant that sacred powers were always present, requiring practitioners to use ritual to explore this magic (Price).

Human existence, and all of life, was subject to the force of wyrd (fate), which was woven by powerful female beings known as the Norns. The Norns resided at the Well of Uror beneath Yggdrasil, and their activity emphasized that while fate was overarching in the realms, it would be influenced by using magical practices. Therefore Galdr arose, with the need to interact with this web to gain foresight, and to secure a favorable outcome to oneself or one’s community (Simek).

The performance of Galdr was integrated into various aspects of life, including healing, aiding in childbirth, and general spell-work. Uses of Galdr can fit into a few different categories.

Healing and Practical Medicine: Galdr chants were performed to ensure safe delivery during childbirth. Additionally it was used to aid against bad weather, disease, or other threats. Incantations could be used to cure sickness, treat wounds, or alleviate pain. Galdr functioned as an applied therapeutic technique in Norse society (Robertson).

Influence over Fate and Circumstance: This category relates the idea of manipulating the flow of events such as altering moods to calm, or incite rage, etc. Luck and success were also common in activities like trading, hunting, or sailing (Price).

Spiritual and Ritual Communication: These Galdr chants directed outward to the spirit world, often requiring someone more skilled to perform. However this means communication addressing gods or ancestors was possible. While the most intense form of prophecy was associated with other magical types, Galdr spells could be part of the ritual preparation or execution, helping induce a trance-like state to provide clarity to any seer (Price).

While both men and women participated in Galdr, the most potent ritual performers were often women known as völur. This translates to “wand carrier” and refers to an incantation or spell of the seeresses. These women specialized in prophecy and the magic of the chants. They also occupied a position of social and ritual power, specializing in Galdr but also other magic forms that allowed them to perform in a ceremonial setting, often accompanied by a staff with symbolic use. This expertise ensured that the most complex fate altering uses of the chanted word were reserved for, and preserved by those with the deepest insight (Price).

The importance of sound and oral culture cannot be overstated though. Since written structure was a minority at best, the transmission of cultural, historical, and sacred knowledge relied almost entirely on oral performance. This includes poetry, sagas, and ritualized chanting. The chanted word was the primary medium through which communal belief was reinforced to engage with the divine. Galdr united people in a shared spiritual experience, linking the human voice to the forces of nature.

Embedded Media

Because no definitive written music survived from the Viking Age, ethnomusicologists and contemporary artists rely on linguistic and archaeological evidence to recreate these ritual sounds. A powerful example is found in the work of Norwegian artist Kjell Braaten, who reconstructs and performs these ancient Norse Galdr chants.

Sonic Analysis

Historical descriptions of Galdr appear in texts like the Poetic Edda and sagas like Eiriks saga, however unfortunately the exact sonic form was never written and only passed down orally. Fortunately though, contemporary artists like Kjell Braaten, attempt to reconstruct these sounds using traditional instruments and environments similar to those used in ritual (Braaten). Braaten's approach to reconstruct Galdr emphasizes the breath driven chanting and drone tones that capture the qualities described in Old Norse sources. Braaten plays live ritual performances where he uses cyclical vocal patterns to evoke what Galdr may have sounded like. Kjell Braaten has explained in multiple interviews that his work is meant to “recreate a connection to our ancestors” and draw inspiration from “old Nordic traditions and the landscapes that shaped them,” grounding Galdr chanting in natural acoustics (Braaten) . In addition to musical reconstruction, an important part of understanding Galdr is recognizing how non-musical sounds and the environment functioned as part of the ritual itself.

The sonic characteristics of Galdr can be broken down into the following primary features.

Texture: The texture of his voice is often dense which is built upon the deep drone tones that are provided either by an instrument or held by vocal tones, over which the chanting voice is layered (Braaten).

Rhythm and Pitch: The rhythmic structure is repetitive, which is crucial to induce a meditative state. Pitch is generally low, which emphasizes a grounding sound for focusing on the physical energy of the chant. They are simple patterns and designed for long ritual durations.

Breath and Phrasing: Braaten’s performances show the physical labor of the chant. It is very difficult, especially for those not musically inclined to deliberately employ the breath chanting Braaten does. This technique embodies a spiritual energy required to manifest intentions associated with Norse rituals.

Soundscape Integration: Performances like these were rarely done in silence. All of the following but not limited to, have shaped the atmosphere of Galdr performances: wind through trees, fire crackling, footsteps on the ground, resonance from a wooden hall (if inside). These are included intentionally as environmental noise becomes part of the soundscape, creating a space that feels alive.

All of this is to mirror the raw and unpolished parts associated with the Norse ritual. By blending intentional sound with environment noise, Braaten reconstructed what Galdr might have felt like for participants.

Religious and Spiritual Significance

Galdr operated as a performance magic deep in the belief that chanting and spoken intention could manifest power into the physical world (Simek). The vibrations and repetitions of the chant were understood as a direct channel of communication with ancestral spirits.

The core function of Galdr was to blend ritual and music to achieve a specific outcome. As a practice, it was adaptable, meaning the runes, focus, and energy are able to be changed to align with an intended outcome, whether it was healing or fortune. The practice involved a focus on the physical vibration of the chant, reinforcing the belief that the sound itself was actively reshaping reality.

Runes were believed to possess intrinsic, concentrated magical power (Price). Each was linked to a specific force or concept such as protection or victory. The Galdr practitioner would chant the name or sound of the specific rune associated with their magical goal. This act would turn the voice into a conduit of powerful magic. The vibrating sounds of Galdr serve as an amplification to activate the power within the runic symbol. Evidence of these runic charms are found in literary sources like the Poetic Edda, which details Odin’s mastery of the eighteen charms, many of which are explicitly linked to the cavitation of runic power.

Modern reconstructions, such as those by Braaten, represent a connection to pre-Christian Norse spirituality (Nielsen). They serve as a reflection of preserving heritage and creating new forms of belief through the medium of sound. By invoking the same energy, modern interpretations highlight the continuity of the ancient sonic practice, demonstrating how sound carries faith across centuries. The persistence of Galdr in modern music illustrates an enduring human drive to connect with the past through music.

Conclusion

Galdr stands as a powerful demonstration of sound as a sacred practice in Viking cosmology. It was the way through which the voice and the spirit merge, allowing Galdr practitioners to exert an influence over their own fate. Galdr was multi-sensory and relied on participation, chanting, unique sonic qualities (drones, cyclical rhythm), and the environmental soundscape all combined to create a field of ritual magic. Kjell Braaten’s reconstructions successfully highlight the continuity between his ancient sound and modern spiritual experiences.
